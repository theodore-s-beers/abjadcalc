Usage Notes

  • This follows the the common "eastern" convention. If you're working with Maghribi or otherwise nonconformist sources, then you're likely to get incorrect results. Sorry! I may try to add options at some point. (See below for more detail on the exact rules being observed.)
  • Feel free to include spaces between words; they won't affect the calculation.
  • More broadly, this program is designed to ignore any character that it doesn't recognize. If you enter text that contains one or more unrecognized characters, you'll see a brief error message, followed by the total abjad value of the parts that were counted (i.e., our best guess). It may be correct nonetheless…
  • It's probably best not to include diacritics, or a dagger alif, or anything else outside of the "full letters." Only the latter are counted in the abjad system, anyway, so why add complication?
  • If you're confident that you entered valid text in Arabic or Persian script, but you still received an error message, then please let me know by email. It might be a bug! (There are guaranteed to be some at this stage.)

Specific Rules Followed

  • The basics of abjad numerals are covered in this Wikipedia entry, and they are observed here faithfully. If one digs a bit deeper, however, questions and ambiguities may arise…
  • The treatment of the four "Persian letters" (p, ch, zh, g) is actually well established; it's explained in the same Wikipedia entry. You should encounter no problem using Persian text. (Urdu? Pashto? You're on your own, at least for the time being.)
  • This program treats the Arabic letter tāʾ marbūṭah as if it were a hāʾ and assigns it a value of 5. This is due to a general principle in abjad calculation, that we judge letters by their form, rather than by their pronunciation or other factors.
  • Similarly, alif maqṣūrah is considered equivalent to yāʾ and assigned a value of 10. (Deal with it!)
  • Any full-letter form of hamzah or alif will be assigned a value of 1. This includes alif maddah, which some people have treated as a "double hamzah" with a value of 2. Here we should keep in mind another general principle of abjad numerals: each letter is counted exactly once. Just as shaddah is not taken into consideration, nor should alif maddah be granted double value.
